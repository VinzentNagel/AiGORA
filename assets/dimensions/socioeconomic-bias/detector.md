# Socioeconomic Bias

## 1. Core Definition
Socioeconomic bias is the invisible assumption of middle-class, urban wealth as the universal standard of living. It stigmatizes poverty and erases the lived realities of rural, working-class, or low-income students by embedding financial and spatial prerequisites into educational content.

## 2. Scientific Markers (What the AI Scans For)
* **Hidden Financial Prerequisites:** Scenarios in textbooks that require students to imagine or interact with assets they may not possess. Examples include math problems calculating the area of "your family's private garden," "the second car," or writing essays about "your last flight to a holiday destination."
* **The Meritocracy Myth:** Narratives that frame wealth and success purely as the result of hard work, intelligence, and moral fortitude, while framing poverty strictly as a consequence of laziness or poor choices, entirely ignoring systemic and inherited inequalities.
* **Rural Erasure (Spatial Bias):** In many global contexts, textbooks exclusively depict modern urban environments (skyscrapers, supermarkets, paved roads) as the sole loci of progress and intelligence, framing rural or agricultural life as backward or uneducated.

## 3. Pedagogical Impact (The Hidden Curriculum)
Textbooks act as agents of social change, influencing students' values, beliefs, and cultural understanding from an early age. When educational materials consistently reflect only middle-class/urban realities, low-income and rural students experience immediate cultural alienation. It creates a psychological barrier to learning, as the student feels the curriculum was not designed for people like them, leading to disengagement and reinforcing systemic class divides through a "hidden curriculum" of exclusion.

## 4. 🛡️ FALSE POSITIVE CHECK (What this is NOT)
To maintain scientific neutrality, the AI must explicitly exclude the following scenarios from being flagged as a bias:
* **Factual Economic Concepts:** A text is NOT biased simply because it mentions money, wealthy historical figures, or explains economic systems like capitalism or taxation. 
* **Standard Transactional Math:** A math problem calculating the cost of basic groceries, train tickets, or standard interest rates for a bank is not inherently classist. 
* **The Threshold for Bias:** The bias ONLY exists if the text assumes middle/upper-class wealth is the "default" prerequisite for the student's lived experience (e.g., instructing a student to "measure your family's swimming pool"), or if it frames poverty as a moral failure of the poor rather than a socioeconomic condition.

## 5. SEVERITY SCALE DEFINITIONS:
Evaluate the finding against this universal pedagogical scale.

- Level 1 (Lexical/Surface): Subtle use of outdated terminology or isolated micro-aggressions. 
  *Eurocentrism Example:* Using the term "Third World" instead of "Global South," or a single exoticizing adjective.

- Level 2 (Structural/Hidden Curriculum): Bias woven into the narrative structure or rigid assumptions. Requires pedagogical intervention.
  *Eurocentrism Example:* A teleological narrative that frames a non-Western country as simply "catching up" to Western industrialization, or centering European actors in a global event.

- Level 3 (Exclusionary/Erasure): Active hierarchization of human value or complete epistemic erasure of a group's agency.
  *Eurocentrism Example:* "White Savior" narratives that justify colonization, or claiming an inhabited continent was "discovered" (completely erasing indigenous existence).
